What Is Meditation? And What Is It Not? For many people, the word "meditation" conjures up images of hippies in tie-dyed shirts or Buddhist monks dressed in orange sitting cross-legged, forefingers clasped in a circle, while chanting "ohm." Incense sticks and sounds bowls may be thrown in there too. Let's look more closely at some of the stereotypes and misconceptions associated with meditation Many think of meditation as a religious activity associated with Buddhism, Hinduism, and other religions. While many practitioners of these religions do, in fact, practice meditation, meditation itself is not inherently religious. Modern mindfulness meditation is a fancy way of paying attention to what's going on right now, in this moment. It's also concerned with working toward accepting what's going on right here, right now without wanting to change anything. It asks the questions: What dol see? What do I hear? What do l feel? What sensations do I have in my body? It is an exercise in concentration and acceptance, and is a way to build these muscles in the brain. There is nothing inherently religious about it.
